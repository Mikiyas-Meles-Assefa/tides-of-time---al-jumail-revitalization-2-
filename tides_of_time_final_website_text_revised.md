# Tides of Time

## Reimagining Al Jumail as a Living Memory

**Project type:** Immersive heritage experience\
**Site:** Al Jumail, northern coast of Qatar\
**Coordinates:** 25.97°N / 51.10°E

Tides of Time proposes the revitalization of Al Jumail Village as a living heritage site. The project transforms an abandoned coastal settlement into an active cultural landscape where visitors learn through participation, conservation, and guided experience.

Rather than presenting heritage as something to observe from a distance, the proposal enables visitors to engage with everyday life: pearling, trade, family life, hospitality, limited agriculture, and collective memory.

---

# The Site

## A Coastal Settlement in Decline

Al Jumail is a coastal village in northern Qatar, established in the 18th–19th century and inhabited until the mid-20th century. It was historically supported by pearling and fishing.

Today, the site is abandoned. Coral-stone buildings remain in varying states of decay, shaped by wind, salt, and time.

The site represents both physical deterioration and a weakening connection between contemporary society and pre-oil ways of life.

---

# Historical Context

## From Maritime Life to Abandonment

### 18th–19th Century: Settlement

Al Jumail develops as a small coastal community dependent on pearling and fishing.

### Early 20th Century: Economic Shift

The decline of natural pearl markets affects coastal settlements across the Gulf.

### Mid 20th Century: Migration

Population shifts toward urban centers following the rise of the oil economy.

### Present: Abandonment

The village remains as a historical site with significant cultural value but limited active use.

---

# The Challenge

## A Measurable Heritage Risk

The site faces three primary risks:

1. Structural decay of coral-stone buildings due to salt, humidity, and erosion
2. Loss of intangible heritage, including oral history and daily practices
3. Limited public engagement with pre-oil coastal life

Without intervention, both the physical site and its cultural meaning will continue to deteriorate.

---

# Research Question

## Central Inquiry

How can the abandoned village of Al Jumail be revitalized into a sustainable, interactive Live Museum that preserves Qatar’s heritage while meeting the social and environmental standards of the 21st century?

---

# Core Questions

1. How can Al Jumail be adapted into a public heritage site without damaging its fragile fabric?
2. How can visitors actively engage with history instead of passively observing it?
3. How can environmental constraints guide, rather than limit, design decisions?
4. How can conservation, design, and policy operate as a single system?

---

# Project Approach

## From Preservation to Use

The project shifts from static preservation to controlled activation.

- **Preservation** protects the existing structures
- **Adaptation** introduces low-impact uses
- **Participation** allows visitors to experience daily life practices

The goal is not reconstruction, but careful reuse.

---

# Core Vision

## A Living Heritage Site

Al Jumail becomes a site where heritage is experienced through activity.

Three guiding principles:

- **Interaction:** Visitors engage with tools, spaces, and processes
- **Participation:** Activities reflect real practices
- **Everyday Life:** Focus on work, family, and social interaction

---

# The Intervention

## Site Strategy

The proposal introduces a network of adapted structures. Each building hosts a specific theme linked to historical daily life.

### 01. Pearling House

Focus: Maritime labor and pearling practices

- Oyster opening demonstrations
- Diving tools and weights
- Pearl grading explanation

### 02. Family Life House

Focus: Domestic space and daily routines

- Interior layout interpretation
- Textile weaving (sadu)
- Food preparation context

### 03. Trade House

Focus: Local exchange systems

- Display of goods (fish, dates, pearls)
- Barter-based interaction
- Historic currency references

This house reflects Al Jumail as a regional exchange point, not a large-scale international hub.

### 04. Majlis House

Focus: Social gathering and hospitality

- Seating and spatial arrangement
- Arabic coffee preparation
- Storytelling sessions

### 05. Agriculture Experience House

Focus: Resource-limited cultivation

- Date palm uses
- Small-scale planting techniques
- Water-use awareness

Agriculture is presented as constrained and adaptive, reflecting local environmental conditions.

### 06. Art & Memory House

Focus: Reflection and documentation

- Community memory wall
- Archival material
- Visitor contributions

### 07. Garden & Greenhouse

Focus: Contemporary sustainability interpretation

- Demonstration planting
- Hydroponic cultivation systems adapted to arid, saline conditions
- Water-efficient closed-loop irrigation

This zone introduces hydroponics as a contemporary response to the environmental limits of the site, without presenting it as a historical practice.

### 08. Food Space

Focus: Local cuisine

- Simple, locally inspired meals
- Outdoor seating integrated with the site

### 09. Service Area

Focus: Operations

- Staff circulation
- Maintenance and logistics

---

# Visitor Experience

The visitor journey follows a clear route through the six main houses, beginning with the Pearling House near the shoreline and moving through trade, family life, and social spaces before ending at the Art & Memory House.

Visitors move along defined paths supported by staff guidance, ensuring both accessibility and protection of fragile structures.

The experience prioritizes clarity, movement, and gradual understanding rather than spectacle.

# Implementation

## Phase 01: Documentation

- Conduct detailed site surveys and structural assessments of all buildings
- Map fragile zones and areas suitable for intervention
- Collect oral histories from tribal descendants to ensure cultural accuracy
- Coordinate with Qatar Museums and researchers to establish conservation guidelines

## Phase 02: Stabilization

- Reinforce unstable coral-stone walls using approved conservation methods
- Define restricted and accessible zones across the site
- Implement basic infrastructure for safety (paths, barriers, signage)
- Ensure all work aligns with environmental and heritage regulations

## Phase 03: Adaptation

- Convert selected houses into activity spaces with minimal physical intervention
- Install low-impact elements (tools, seating, shading) without altering original structures
- Introduce greenhouse and hydroponic systems in designated non-sensitive areas
- Set up staff zones and service routes to support operations without disrupting the site

## Phase 04: Operation

- Train staff and guides to manage visitor flow and interpret the site
- Implement controlled visitor routes and capacity limits
- Establish maintenance schedules for structures and environmental monitoring
- Coordinate ongoing oversight with public institutions for long-term management

---

# Feasibility

The project is feasible due to its phased approach, low-impact design, and alignment with existing institutions.

- **Institutional support:** Qatar Museums and government bodies already manage heritage sites and can lead implementation
- **Low intervention strategy:** The project avoids heavy construction, reducing cost, risk, and approval complexity
- **Existing site condition:** The village is already accessible and partially documented, allowing work to begin with minimal setup
- **Scalable implementation:** Phases allow gradual development based on funding, testing, and feedback
- **Operational viability:** Revenue from visitors, guided tours, and food services can support maintenance and staffing

---

# Impacts

- Preserves a coastal heritage site
- Enables active learning of pre-oil life
- Supports cultural tourism
- Demonstrates contemporary sustainability practices, including hydroponic agriculture as a response to water scarcity
- Raises awareness of environmental constraints

---

# Limitations

- Fragile coral-stone buildings limit how much can be restored or modified
- High visitor traffic can wear down surfaces and weaken structures
- Cars and road access can damage the site and nearby landscape
- Staff housing and service areas risk disrupting the historic setting if not carefully placed
- Salt, wind, and humidity cause ongoing damage and require constant maintenance

---

# Stakeholders

## Tribal Descendants

Provide historical knowledge and cultural validation

Al Jumail was historically inhabited by members of the Al-Kubaisa tribe, alongside families from the Al-Muhannadi confederation—particularly the Al Hassan branch—as well as Al-Bu Kawara and Al-Humaidat families.

## Public Institutions

Lead planning, approval, and implementation

- Qatar Museums: project owner for heritage sites; sets conservation standards, approves interventions, and oversees implementation
- Ministry of Culture: supports programming, interpretation, and cultural alignment
- Ministry of Environment and Climate Change: regulates environmental protection, land use, and sustainability measures
- Relevant public works/municipal bodies: coordinate access, utilities, and site infrastructure within conservation limits

## Researchers and Educators

Support documentation and learning programs

## Private Operators

Manage food and activity programs within constraints

## Visitors

Engage with the site through participation

## Site Staff

Maintain operations and preservation standards

---

# National Alignment

Al Jumail aligns with Qatar’s national strategies by linking heritage preservation with sustainability and public engagement.

- **QNV 2030 – Social Development:** Preserves national heritage and strengthens cultural identity through ancestral memory and community involvement
- **QNV 2030 – Environmental Development:** Promotes responsible use of resources and environmental protection within a fragile coastal setting
- **NDS3 – Quality of Life:** Creates accessible cultural spaces that support education, leisure, and family-oriented experiences
- **NDS3 – Sustainability:** Encourages resource-efficient systems and long-term environmental resilience, including water-conscious design approaches

---

# Conclusion

Tides of Time proposes a clear and feasible approach to revitalizing Al Jumail.

The project does not attempt to reconstruct the past. Instead, it protects what remains and introduces controlled, meaningful uses that allow visitors to understand the site through experience.

Its strength lies in balance: access without damage, interpretation without distortion, and use without loss of authenticity.
