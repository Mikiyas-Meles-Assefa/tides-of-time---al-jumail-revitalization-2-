# Tides of Time

## Reimagining Al Jumail Village as a Living Memory

**Project type:** Immersive heritage experience  
**Site:** Al Jumail, northern coast of Qatar  
**Coordinates:** 25.97°N / 51.10°E  

Tides of Time proposes the revitalization of Al Jumail Village as a living heritage museum. The project transforms an abandoned coastal settlement from a silent ruin into an active cultural landscape where visitors learn through participation, memory, conservation, and design.

Rather than treating heritage as something to be observed from a distance, the proposal turns Al Jumail into a public experience of daily life: pearling, trade, family interiors, majlis culture, agriculture, food, craft, and collective memory.

---

# The Site

## A Ghost on the Northern Coast

Al Jumail is a nineteenth-century pearling and fishing village located on the northern coast of Qatar. Once a vital center of maritime life, it now stands abandoned.

The site represents more than physical ruins. It reflects a disappearing connection between contemporary society and ancestral roots.

### 02. Cultural Erasure

# Core Inquiries

The project is guided by four central questions:

1. How can Al Jumail be transformed into a sustainable and interactive live museum?
2. How can heritage preservation coexist with modern environmental and social demands?
3. How can visitors actively experience, rather than passively observe, cultural history?
4. How can sustainability, architecture, and storytelling be integrated into one system?

---

# The Philosophy

## From Preservation to Participation

The project moves beyond the idea of preservation as static protection. Instead, it proposes participation as a method of keeping memory alive.

The goal is not only to conserve walls, pathways, and ruins. The goal is to reactivate the meanings attached to them through programs, storytelling, environmental care, and community involvement.

---

# Core Vision

## A Living Museum of Daily Life

The central vision is to transform Al Jumail into a living museum where visitors actively experience pre-oil Qatari life through interaction, participation, and daily activities.

The proposal is built around three principles:

- **Interaction:** Visitors engage directly with cultural practices, spaces, and stories.
- **Participation:** Heritage becomes something people do, not only something they look at.
- **Daily Activities:** The museum focuses on everyday life: work, family, food, craft, trade, hospitality, and memory.

---

# The Response

## A Living Experience

The intervention transforms Al Jumail into a living heritage museum where restored village structures host interactive programs about pearling, trade, family life, agriculture, hospitality, and collective memory.

The solution includes:

1. **Stabilized ruins and adaptive reuse**  
   Fragile structures are assessed, protected, and reused only where conservation conditions allow.

2. **Hands-on cultural learning houses**  
   Selected houses become immersive activity spaces focused on pearling, family life, trade, hospitality, agriculture, and memory.

3. **Community-led memory and policy alignment**  
   Descendant families, public institutions, educators, private partners, and site staff each have defined roles in keeping the project accurate, feasible, and responsible.

The proposal changes the site from a silent relic into a vibrant, participatory ecosystem.

# From Vision to Implementation

The project is not only a visual restoration. It is a coordinated system where science protects the site, design makes heritage experiential, and policy makes the work feasible.

### Problem

Decay, cultural erasure, and modernization pressure are weakening Al Jumail's physical and social memory.

### Context

The village represents Qatar's nineteenth-century pearling and fishing life before the oil economy transformed settlement patterns.

### Significance

# Evidence Base

The proposal responds to documented heritage value, visible decay, and national planning priorities.

## Heritage Value

Qatar Museums identifies Al Jumail as a nineteenth-century traditional village with an intact mosque minaret and ancestral importance for local families.

## Historic Transition

Public heritage descriptions place Al Jumail within the wider shift from coastal pearling and fishing settlements toward modern urban growth after the oil and gas economy.

QNV 2030 frames national development through social, human, economic, and environmental pillars, including the balance between modernization, tradition, and environmental protection.

# Science, Design, and Policy Integration

Each discipline answers a different project risk.

## Science: Conservation and Ecology

Because the village sits in a harsh coastal environment, each intervention begins with material assessment. Salt, humidity, wind, erosion, fragile masonry, and water demand are treated as design constraints.

Science guides:

- conservation of fragile ruins
- protection from coastal exposure
- material assessment before adaptive reuse
- water-efficient agriculture
- environmental monitoring
- climate resilience

## Design: Adaptive Reuse and Experience

Design turns selected houses into active learning spaces while keeping ruin character visible. The route connects pearling, trade, family life, agriculture, hospitality, and memory as one story.

Design guides:

- visitor circulation
- house-by-house programming
- interactive exhibits
- before/after visualization
- sensory storytelling
- integration of food, craft, landscape, and memory

## Policy: Governance and National Alignment

Policy gives the project a delivery structure. Heritage authorities, environmental oversight, cultural programming, and QNV 2030 priorities define what can be restored, used, and maintained.

Policy guides:

- institutional approval
- conservation standards
- community participation
- cultural tourism alignment
- environmental protection
- long-term operations

# Feasibility and Implementation Pathway

Feasibility depends on starting with documentation and conservation before adding visitor activity. Programs are introduced only where structures, ecology, access, and staffing can support them.

## Phase 01: Document and Listen

Map the existing ruins, record material conditions, identify climate risks, and collect oral histories from descendant families.

## Phase 02: Stabilize and Protect

Secure fragile walls, define no-touch zones, protect coastal ecology, and prepare conservation guidelines before visitor programming begins.

## Phase 03: Adapt and Program

Fit selected structures with low-impact exhibits, shaded routes, interpretation, workshops, and service access without erasing the historic fabric.

## Phase 04: Operate and Monitor

Launch guided visits, school programs, food and craft partnerships, maintenance schedules, visitor caps, and ongoing environmental monitoring.

# Anticipated Impacts and Limitations

The project is valuable, but it must be carefully managed.

## Anticipated Impacts

- Preserves a visible link to Qatar's pre-oil maritime identity.
- Creates hands-on education through pearling, trade, agriculture, family life, and oral memory.
- Supports sustainable cultural tourism while giving local families and institutions active roles.
- Builds environmental awareness through coastal conservation, water use, and climate resilience.

## Limitations and Risks

- Fragile ruins require expert conservation and continuous maintenance.
- Too many visitors could damage the site or weaken the feeling of authenticity.
- Commercial programs must not overpower cultural memory or community authority.
- Coastal climate exposure means the project needs monitoring, repair budgets, and adaptive management.

---

# Decision Statement

## The Goal

The goal is not to rebuild the past, but to make it legible, protected, and useful for the future.

A successful Al Jumail revitalization would begin with conservation, earn legitimacy through community memory, operate through clear institutional roles, and use design to translate heritage into public learning.

Its value comes from balancing access with restraint: visitors can participate in the story, but the site remains protected as a fragile cultural landscape.

# Stakeholder Landscape

## A Heritage Ecosystem Led by Memory, Conservation, and Shared Enterprise

The project depends on cooperation between community memory holders, public institutions, educators, private partners, visitors, and operational staff.

## Tribal Descendants

**Role:** Memory and cultural accuracy  

Al-Kubaisa and Al-Muhannadi families guide oral-history collection, review interpretive content, and help keep the project rooted in lived ancestral memory.

## Public Institutions

**Role:** Conservation and governance  

Qatar Museums sets conservation standards, the Ministry of Culture supports heritage programming, and environmental authorities protect the coastal setting.

## Education and Research

**Role:** Learning and documentation  

Schools, universities, and researchers use the site as a field classroom for archaeology, ecology, architecture, oral history, and sustainable design.

## Private Partners

**Role:** Operations and enterprise  

Agriculture, food, craft, and hospitality partners operate selected programs while following conservation limits and community-approved storytelling.

## Visitors and Families

**Role:** Public engagement  

Visitors become active learners through pearling, trade, farming, majlis, and memory activities rather than passive observers of abandoned ruins.

## Site Staff

**Role:** Daily management  

Guides, conservators, gardeners, educators, and service teams manage visitor flow, maintenance, safety, interpretation, and long-term monitoring.

# Conclusion

Tides of Time proposes Al Jumail as a living memory site where heritage preservation, environmental care, cultural programming, and public participation work together.

The project is significant because it responds to a real heritage problem: the physical decay of an abandoned village and the cultural fading of Qatar's pre-oil maritime life. Its intervention is coherent because every design element has a role in the larger system: houses become learning spaces, the landscape becomes an environmental classroom, stakeholders become active stewards, and media becomes a tool for explaining the proposal.

The strongest version of the project is not a complete reconstruction. It is a careful, phased revitalization that protects what remains, activates what can be safely used, and gives future visitors a meaningful way to understand Al Jumail's place in Qatar's cultural memory.
